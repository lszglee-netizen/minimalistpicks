I am not the kind of person who tracks heating bills. Then we moved into an old apartment with a front door that opened directly onto a hallway, with no insulation, and a half-inch gap underneath. The first winter, the radiator ran constantly. The corner of the living room nearest the door was permanently 10°F colder than the kitchen.

Second winter, I installed a $15 door draft stopper. Heating bill: down about $80 for the season. The corner of the living room is now within 2°F of the kitchen. The radiator runs less.

I did not believe a piece of foam wrapped in fabric could do this much.

![A draft stopper installed at the base of a wooden interior door](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1631880898728-15d6e8a5c1e0?w=1200&q=80&auto=format&fit=crop)

## How it works

The standard design is a foam tube wrapped in fabric, with two halves that sandwich the bottom of the door — one half on each side. You slide the door through it; it stays in place via tension, no adhesive needed.

When the door is closed, both foam tubes press into the floor on either side. Cold air, dust, light, and most sound have nowhere to come through.

## Why this one

- Installs in 30 seconds — no tools, no glue, no measuring
- The two-sided design seals from both the warm and cold side
- Fits most standard interior and exterior doors with gaps up to ~1"
- Cuts noise transfer noticeably (great for shared walls/rooms)
- Doesn't damage door or floor when removed

## Where it doesn't work

Three real downsides to be aware of:

**Doesn't work well on doors with very large gaps.** If you have more than an inch of clearance under your door (some older homes), this style will sag in the middle and not seal properly. Get the heavier "sweep" style that screws to the bottom of the door instead.

**The fabric drags on carpet/rugs over time.** If your door opens onto a rug, opening and closing pulls the fabric across the rug. It still works fine — it just gets dirty on the underside and may show wear after a year. If you hate that, buy a draft stopper that snaps off and back on instead of the slide-through style.

**Don't expect it to fix a drafty house.** This blocks air going under one door. If your windows leak, your attic isn't insulated, and your fireplace damper is open — the draft stopper is a small fix on top of bigger problems. Worth doing anyway, but don't expect miracles.

> Energy upgrades are almost always investments — you spend money to save money. This one pays for itself in roughly four weeks of winter heating.

## A surprise benefit

Two unexpected wins from installing one on our bedroom door:

1. **Light bleed dropped to near-zero.** A 1mm strip of light under the bedroom door (from the hallway) used to wake me up. It's gone now. Sleep noticeably better.

2. **Pet hair migration slowed dramatically.** Our cat used to leave a fine trail of hair under every closed door. The draft stopper acts as a sweep — keeps the hair on her side of the door.

## Who shouldn't buy

If you rent in a warm climate (Texas summers, Florida year-round), heating savings won't apply and the draft is air conditioning escaping, which is a less obvious daily problem. Still helpful, but the math is weaker.

If you own a house and are planning real weatherstripping anyway (a few hours, $30 in materials), do that. It's better and permanent.

For everyone else: $15, 30 seconds to install, real measurable savings over a single winter. One of the rare cases where a small purchase produces a number you can actually point to.
