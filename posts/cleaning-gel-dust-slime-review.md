I was skeptical. The product is, literally, a green blob of squishy goo in a plastic tub. It looks like a child's toy. The marketing claims it cleans keyboards, car vents, electronic equipment, and tight crevices — which is a long way of saying it claims to do something normal cleaning products cannot.

It does. Embarrassingly well. I am writing this with a keyboard I cleaned six minutes ago, after eight months of accumulated crumbs, dust, and hair came out in two minutes.

![A blob of bright green cleaning gel being pressed onto a computer keyboard](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1611078489935-0cb964de46d6?w=1200&q=80&auto=format&fit=crop)

## How it works

You take a wad of the gel — about the size of a golf ball — and press it gently onto whatever you want to clean. It sinks into gaps, between keys, into vent slats. When you lift it off, everything that was loose down there comes up with it.

The gel itself is mildly tacky but doesn't leave residue on hard surfaces. It picks up dust, crumbs, hair, lint, and (this is the gross part) a startling amount of what is presumably skin and oil from frequently-touched surfaces.

## Why this one

- Cleans places brushes physically can't reach (between keys, inside vents)
- Reusable maybe 30–50 times before it gets too dirty to bother
- One $7 tub lasts about 4–6 months for one person
- Works on keyboards, car interiors, phone cases, fans, sewing machines, instrument keys
- No batteries, no chemicals, no power tools

## The downsides nobody mentions

I want to flag these specifically because they're absent from most product descriptions:

**It dries out if you don't seal the tub properly.** The single biggest reason for one-star reviews. The gel arrives in a clamshell or screw-top container — close it firmly after every use. Left open for two days in a dry climate, it turns into a stiff useless lump. There's no reviving it.

**It can leave a slight oily film on some surfaces.** Tested on Apple Magic Keyboard: no residue. Tested on certain mechanical keyboard PBT keycaps: a light film that needed a microfiber wipe. Test on a small corner first if you have unusual materials.

**Once it picks up enough dirt, it turns dark and looks repulsive.** This is normal — it's literally dirt. But people freak out and assume it's "expired." It's not expired; it's just done its job. Throw it out and buy another one. At $7 a tub, this is not a problem.

> The gel doesn't do anything magic. It just gets into places nothing else can. That's enough.

## Where it shines

The two killer use cases, in my experience:

1. **Car air vents.** The slats trap dust the vacuum can't reach. Press the gel in, peel it off, watch the gunk come out. Repeat for each vent. Total time: 5 minutes per car.

2. **Mechanical keyboards.** The crumbs that fall between the keys are essentially impossible to remove any other way short of disassembly. The gel pulls them straight up.

Less great for: very flat surfaces (a microfiber cloth is faster), anything porous (fabric, paper), or sticky messes (it'll grab the sticky and itself become useless).

Seven dollars, lasts months, fixes a specific problem nothing else fixes. The bar for a "good buy" is "does it pay for itself in saved frustration." This one pays for itself in twenty minutes.
