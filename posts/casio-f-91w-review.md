The Casio F-91W has been in continuous production since 1989. That is older than most of the people who buy one today. The design has not changed. The price has barely moved. The watch on my wrist right now is mechanically and visually identical to one a kid wore to school the year the Berlin Wall came down.

I bought mine because I was tired of charging an Apple Watch every night, tired of glancing at it during conversations, tired of treating a wristwatch like a phone. Four years later, I still wear it every day.

![A black plastic digital watch resting on a wooden desk next to a notebook](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1523275335684-37898b6baf30?w=1200&q=80&auto=format&fit=crop)

## What it is

A 38mm plastic-resin digital quartz watch. About 21 grams on the wrist — light enough to forget. A daily alarm, an hourly chime, a stopwatch, a backlight, and an auto-calendar that runs to 2099. Water resistance to 30 meters, which in real life means "swim, shower, do dishes; don't scuba dive."

That's the whole feature list. There is no app. There is no Bluetooth. There is no heart rate sensor. You wind nothing. You charge nothing.

## Why this one

- **Battery life is approximately seven years.** Mine is on its original battery at year four, still going.
- **It weighs nothing.** 21 grams. You will forget you're wearing it within an hour.
- **The display is always on.** No wrist-flick to wake. No "tap to see the time." You glance, you know.
- **Quartz-accurate.** Drift is about 30 seconds a month — within atomic-clock distance for daily life.
- **Universally legible.** Big digits, high contrast, readable in any light without thinking.
- **It costs $15–$20.** The exact price of a paperback book.

## What four years of wear actually looks like

The watch on my wrist has earned its scratches. The resin case has fine hairline scuffs across the bezel from doorframes and gym equipment. The screen — actually a thin acrylic crystal — has two scratches I can see if I tilt it, and zero I can see when I'm reading the time. Nothing has cracked. Nothing has loosened. The buttons still click cleanly.

The strap is original. Casio's resin straps are famously the first thing to fail on these watches — they get brittle and snap at the spring bar after two to four years of daily wear. I'm overdue. When mine goes, a replacement OEM strap is $5 on Amazon, or you can fit a cheap 18mm NATO strap if you don't mind the lugs being a little short.

## What it isn't

I want to be honest about the things people don't mention in the love letters.

**The backlight is bad.** It's a single orange LED that lights the display for about 1.5 seconds when you press the upper-left button. In a dark room, it works. In a movie theater, it works. As a "checking the time without waking my partner" tool — it works, just barely. It is not the bright EL backlight on the slightly-more-expensive F-105 or A158.

**Every button press beeps.** There is no way to turn this off. In a quiet meeting, setting the alarm or checking the date will produce a small, audible chirp. I've stopped caring. Your colleagues might not.

**The alarm will not wake a heavy sleeper.** The beeper is small and the alarm runs for 20 seconds. It works if you're already a light sleeper or wake naturally near that time. If you currently sleep through three iPhone alarms, this will not save you.

**The hourly chime is on by default.** First thing you should do when you unbox one is turn it off (hold ADJUST until the time blinks, press MODE once, then press the lower-right button to toggle SIG). Otherwise it beeps every hour, forever.

**The stopwatch only counts to 59:59.99.** Anything past an hour, you're out of luck. Fine for cooking, intervals, and most workouts. Not fine for a marathon.

**Battery replacement is a small ordeal.** The case back has four tiny screws. A jeweler will charge $15–$25 to swap the CR2016 — more than the watch cost. You can do it yourself with a #00 Phillips and a $3 battery, but it's fiddly and you will worry about the gasket.

## Who it's actually for

People who want to know what time it is, and nothing else.

If you want notifications on your wrist, this is the wrong watch. If you want to track sleep, runs, or your heart, this is the wrong watch. If you wear suits regularly, this is — depending on your taste — either the wrong watch or, paradoxically, the perfect one. (There is a small, persistent subculture of people who wear an F-91W with a tuxedo.)

For everyone else — for students, engineers, programmers, parents, anyone who spends their day in front of a screen and would like one fewer screen on their body — this is a small, quiet, near-perfect object.

> The F-91W is a watch designed in 1989 for a problem that has only gotten worse: how to know the time without being interrupted by everything else.

## The bigger picture

I've replaced almost every smart device in my life over the past decade. The watch on my wrist is the only thing I've worn for four straight years that I have neither thought about replacing nor charged a single time.

Twenty dollars. Made in a Casio factory in Thailand. Older than half the apps on your phone. It is, by a wide margin, the most cost-effective object I own.
